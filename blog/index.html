Motivations

I have always enjoyed reading and writing and thought I would pursue a degree in comparative literature. Some topics I hold dearly are sci-fi, historical fictions, as well as Asian diaspora literatures. In highschool, I began mesmerized by neuroscience through reading books by Oliver Sacks and other science fiction where brain-computer interactions are common. Subsequently, I chose neuroscience as my major area of study, and psychology and computer science as tools/persecptives to understand the brain. People who knew me from childhood were surprised by this transition, and I have been seeking a deeper explanation for entering into science myself.

I think I found an answer through one of the lunches with seminar speakers at Hopkins Neuroscience with Dr. Catherine DuLac. She mentioned how her initial interests in literature, especially how different charaters' motives promote the plots, resemble interactions of molecules and brain regions that inform our daily behaviors. This quote, although not exact, has been in my heart and becomes my hidden motive to study how distributed neural circuits across multiple brain regions coordinate activity and communicate with each other to process information and promote our understanding of the world.

In college, I was also able to fit classes such as media analysis and digital anthropology into my schedule, and led a project aiming to render anthropology artifacts in virtual reality. These classes and experiences allow me to develop multi-media skills to express and reflect my journey. I hope to continue my passion with writing and arts in this space.

I would also like to dedicate the blogs to the Asian as well as other international students communities. They provide me with courage and strength while studying abroad under the current political tensions. Writing in my mother language allows me to express myself most accurately and gives me the greatest inner peace among all things, so some of the blogs will be presented in Chinese with an English abstract.
